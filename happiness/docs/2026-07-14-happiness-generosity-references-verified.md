# Verified citations for Post 09 "The Most Generous Country on Earth Isn't the Happiest"

Checked 2026-07-14 against primary/authoritative sources. Rule: verified or omit. The
constraints below are binding on the prose.

## The data source (authority)

Helliwell, J. F., Layard, R., Sachs, J. D., De Neve, J.-E., Aknin, L. B., & Wang, S. (Eds.).
(2024). *World Happiness Report 2024*. University of Oxford, Wellbeing Research Centre.
https://www.worldhappiness.report/ed/2024/
Supports: the Cantril ladder (respondents rate their life 0 = worst possible to 10 = best
possible); "Generosity" is the residual of regressing the national average response to "Have
you donated money to a charity in the past month?" on LOG GDP per capita (so it is
income-adjusted giving, not raw dollars); the six factors. CONSTRAINTS: call these six people
the EDITORS, not authors. Keep "log" if quoting the generosity formula. Published by the
Wellbeing Research Centre (University of Oxford) with Gallup and the UN SDSN.

## Generosity corroboration (World Giving Index)

Charities Aid Foundation. (2023). *World Giving Index 2023: Global trends in generosity*.
Charities Aid Foundation. https://www.cafonline.org/about-us/publications/2023-publications/caf-world-giving-index-2023
Supports: the CAF World Giving Index ranked Indonesia the world's most generous country.
CONFIRMED editions with Indonesia at #1: 2018, 2021, 2022, 2023. CONSTRAINT: do NOT state a
"six consecutive years" streak as fact (that is CAF's own phrasing and does not map cleanly to
annual editions; the 2019 release was a decade-aggregate where Indonesia was not #1, and there
was no standard 2020 edition). Safe wording: "topped the World Giving Index in the 2018, 2021,
2022, and 2023 editions." If you use "sixth year running," attribute it explicitly to CAF.

## The evaluated-vs-experienced distinction

Kahneman, D., & Deaton, A. (2010). High income improves evaluation of life but not emotional
well-being. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 107*(38), 16489-16493.
https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1011492107
Supports: the distinction between EVALUATIVE well-being (how you judge your life, the Cantril
ladder) and EXPERIENCED / emotional well-being (day-to-day affect). CONSTRAINT: cite ONLY for
this distinction. Do NOT cite the specific "~$75,000 satiation" income threshold, which later
work (Killingsworth 2021; Killingsworth, Kahneman & Mellers 2023) substantially revised. The
distinction itself is sound.

## Framing note

Descriptive/associational essay, not life or policy advice. Within-data results (Indonesia's
ranks, the generosity-to-ladder correlation of 0.055, the affect correlations) are computed from

carry an APA 7 reference. Culture (communal/religious giving) is context, not a proven cause.
