# Verified citations for Post 07 "What Actually Moves Your Mental Health"

Checked 2026-07-12 against primary sources (web verification). The dataset is Kaggle; the
productivity/economic figures and the "levers are real" anchors are external.

## Dataset

Singh, H. (2026). *Mental health prediction dataset* [Data set]. Kaggle.
https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/harpartapsingh13/mental-health-prediction-dataset
Supports: 500 records, 18 columns, CC0. Simulated / "real-world inspired" lifestyle data
(the creator's phrase is "real-world inspired," not literally "synthetic"; the perfectly
balanced labels and their reconstructability from the inputs are what show it is generated).

## Productivity cost (chart 3, left figure)

World Health Organization. (2024, September 2). *Mental health at work.*
https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/mental-health-at-work
Exact wording: "Globally, an estimated 12 billion working days are lost every year to
depression and anxiety at a cost of US$ 1 trillion per year in lost productivity."
Supports: the ~US$1 trillion/year lost-productivity figure (and the 12 billion working days).

## Treatment return on investment (chart 3, right figure)

World Health Organization. (2016, April 13). *Investing in treatment for depression and
anxiety leads to fourfold return.*
https://www.who.int/news/item/13-04-2016-investing-in-treatment-for-depression-and-anxiety-leads-to-fourfold-return
Exact wording: "Every US$ 1 invested in scaling up treatment for depression and anxiety leads
to a return of US$ 4 in better health and ability to work."
NOTE: the "US$4" is WHO's rounded restatement. The underlying study reports benefit-to-cost
ratios of 2.3-3.0:1 (economic benefits only) and 3.3-5.7:1 (including the value of health
returns). Attribute the "$4" to WHO, and use WHO's phrase "better health and ability to work."

Chisholm, D., Sweeny, K., Sheehan, P., Rasmussen, B., Smit, F., Cuijpers, P., & Saxena, S.
(2016). Scaling-up treatment of depression and anxiety: A global return on investment
analysis. *The Lancet Psychiatry, 3*(5), 415-424.
https://doi.org/10.1016/S2215-0366(16)30024-4
Supports: the underlying ROI study behind WHO's "$4" restatement.

## "The levers are real" anchors (honesty section)

Madsen, I. E. H., Nyberg, S. T., Magnusson Hanson, L. L., Ferrie, J. E., Ahola, K.,
Alfredsson, L., Batty, G. D., Bjorner, J. B., Borritz, M., Burr, H., Chastang, J.-F.,
de Graaf, R., Dragano, N., Hamer, M., Jokela, M., Knutsson, A., Koskenvuo, M., Koskinen, A.,
Leineweber, C., ... Kivimaki, M. (2017). Job strain as a risk factor for clinical depression:
Systematic review and meta-analysis with additional individual participant data.
*Psychological Medicine, 47*(8), 1342-1356. https://doi.org/10.1017/S003329171600355X
Supports: work strain / work-life balance is a real risk factor for depression.

Harandi, T. F., Taghinasab, M. M., & Nayeri, T. D. (2017). The correlation of social support
with mental health: A meta-analysis. *Electronic Physician, 9*(9), 5212-5222.
https://doi.org/10.19082/5212
Supports: social support is robustly associated with better mental health.

Schuch, F. B., Vancampfort, D., Firth, J., Rosenbaum, S., Ward, P. B., Silva, E. S.,
Hallgren, M., Ponce de Leon, A., Dunn, A. L., Deslandes, A. C., Fleck, M. P., Carvalho, A. F.,
& Stubbs, B. (2018). Physical activity and incident depression: A meta-analysis of
prospective cohort studies. *The American Journal of Psychiatry, 175*(7), 631-648.
https://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ajp.2018.17111194
Supports: physical activity really does lower depression risk. IMPORTANT for honesty: our
adjusted analysis shows exercise's apparent effect is mostly shared variance with balance and
support; this meta-analysis is why we must NOT say exercise "does not matter."

## Framing note

Analytical essay, not medical or financial advice. All within-dataset relationships are
associational; the data is cross-sectional and simulated, so it cannot establish direction
(the mental-health-and-productivity chicken-and-egg) or causation. The productivity/economic
figures are global external estimates, not computed from this dataset.
